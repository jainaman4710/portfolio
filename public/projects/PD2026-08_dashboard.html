Non-Contact Pulmonary Function Testing

Every year, millions of patients cannot undergo standard lung function testing because the device requires a mouthpiece. We built a system that removes the mouthpiece entirely, using radar to measure chest wall motion instead.

What we built

A 77 GHz mmWave radar device mounted above a supine patient that tracks chest wall displacement to 0.01 mm precision, extracts standard spirometry parameters (FEV1, FVC, PEF) through a six-stage signal processing and machine learning pipeline, and stages lung disease severity in real time against international clinical standards. No mouthpiece. No consumables. No physical contact.

01 — Background

What is a Pulmonary Function Test?

Before explaining what we built, it helps to understand the test we are replacing. Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs) are the primary tool for diagnosing and monitoring respiratory conditions like asthma, COPD, and cystic fibrosis. The most common form is spirometry: the patient inhales maximally, then blows as hard and fast as possible into a flow-sensing mouthpiece. The resulting trace yields several critical measurements.

Key measurements

FVC
Forced Vital Capacity
Total volume forcefully exhaled after a maximum inhale. Reduced in both obstructive and restrictive lung disease.
FEV1
Forced Expiratory Volume, 1 sec
Volume exhaled in the first second. The single most reproducible and clinically decisive spirometry index.
FEV1/FVC
Tiffeneau Index
Gold standard ratio. Below 0.70 indicates obstructive disease (asthma, COPD). Normal or elevated suggests restriction.
PEF
Peak Expiratory Flow
Maximum speed of exhalation. A shallow PEF is a hallmark of obstructed airways in asthma and COPD.

How it works today

Spirometry
Patient inhales maximally, then blows as hard and fast as possible into a mouthpiece connected to a flow sensor.
The mouthpiece
A disposable tube forming an airtight seal with the patient's lips. Every test generates a biohazardous single-use component.
The requirement
The patient must be conscious, co-operative, and physically able to form a lip seal and produce a maximal forced effort.
Why it matters
COPD is the third leading cause of death globally. Early spirometry detects sub-clinical damage years before irreversible decline begins.

Where conventional spirometry breaks down

The mouthpiece that makes spirometry work is also the reason it fails the populations who need it most. These are not edge cases: they represent a large fraction of patients at highest risk from respiratory disease.

😷

Infection risk at every test

Mouthpieces harbour SARS-CoV-2 and M. tuberculosis. The forced exhalation required by spirometry aerosolises pathogens at high concentration, demanding full decontamination between every patient and, during outbreaks, making the test clinically untenable.

👶

Children under 6 have no test

Pediatric asthma and cystic fibrosis are diagnosed in the years before a child can reliably blow into a mouthpiece on command. Without early detection, sub-clinical damage accumulates unchecked until symptoms are severe enough to require other, more invasive investigations.

🛏️

Bedridden patients cannot be tested

ICU patients, post-surgical patients, and the frail elderly are precisely the people most at risk of rapid lung function decline, and precisely the people for whom a clinic visit or even sitting upright to blow into a tube is physically impossible.

🏥

Mass screening is impractical

Factory workers, smokers, and residents of high-pollution cities need regular screening. The per-test cost of disposables, sterilisation time, and trained technician availability makes large-scale screening economically and logistically prohibitive.

03 — The Solution

Removing the mouthpiece by reading the chest wall itself

When you exhale, your chest wall moves. The larger and faster the breath, the larger and faster the movement. Instead of measuring airflow through a tube, we measure the motion of the chest wall from a distance using a radar that can resolve displacements smaller than a human hair. No physical contact is needed at any point in the test.

Core idea

The kinematic signature of forced exhalation is encoded in the way the chest surface accelerates, decelerates, and plateaus. A 77 GHz radar emitting and receiving microwave pulses can detect this motion to 0.01 mm precision. By processing the phase shift of the returning signal, we reconstruct a continuous displacement waveform that encodes the same diagnostic information as a conventional spirometry trace.

📡

What the radar actually measures

The radar does not measure airflow speed. It measures how far the chest surface has physically moved, thousands of times per second. All standard spirometric parameters are derived computationally from this displacement record.

🛏️

Why the patient lies down

A supine patient has far less random body movement than one sitting upright, meaning a cleaner signal. The device mounts directly above the torso, perpendicular to the chest surface, eliminating the angular measurement bias present in angled desktop configurations.

04 — Sensing Physics

Why 77 GHz and how phase-based sensing works

Standard radar measures how long a signal takes to bounce back (time-of-flight) to compute distance. Our system reads the angular phase of the returning wave instead. Phase changes continuously with even the tiniest displacement, giving far finer resolution than time-of-flight at this range. The choice of 77 GHz over the more common 24 GHz band is driven by three compounding advantages.

x(t) = λ · φ(t) ⁄ 4π
Displacement x is directly proportional to phase change φ. At 77 GHz, wavelength λ = 3.9 mm, so a full 2π phase cycle corresponds to just 1.95 mm of chest movement. Sampling phase at the chirp repetition frequency reconstructs a continuous displacement waveform with sensitivity down to 0.01 mm, sufficient to capture the fine acceleration signature used to infer FEV1 and PEF.

Why range resolution is decisive

At 60 cm resolution, the radar cannot distinguish the patient from the chair, the nearby equipment, or the person standing behind them. At 3.7 cm, a software range gate selects exclusively the chest surface at 0.5–1.0 m and zeros all other reflections. On top of this, the on-chip MIMO array uses digital beamforming to steer focus toward the thoracic midline, rejecting arm and head movement artefacts that would otherwise corrupt the lung volume estimate.

05 — Physical Setup

How the system is deployed in practice

The entire system lives in a single sealed enclosure mounted on a mobile stand or ceiling gantry directly above the patient. The patient lies still; no technician interaction with the patient is required after positioning.

1
Sealed enclosure (IP54)

Houses the TI IWR6843ISK radar SoC, Raspberry Pi CM4, medical-grade PSU, and 7″ touchscreen. HDPE radome window allows <0.3 dB transmission loss at 77 GHz. Interior conductive coating prevents CM4 clock harmonics from contaminating radar phase readings.

2
HDPE radome window

Thickness tuned to n·λ⁄2. Prevents internal reflections that would create false targets in the range FFT while maintaining structural integrity and wipeable clinical finish.

3
Software range gate

A digital "box" isolates only the torso reflection at 0.5–1.0 m. The bed surface, floor, and nearby equipment fall outside the gate and are zeroed. MIMO beamforming further suppresses arm and head motion artefacts.

4
Supine patient, zero contact

The patient requires no mouthpiece, no lip seal, and no active co-operation beyond lying still. Reduced macro-motion in the supine position raises SNR and produces more stable FVC readings than an upright seated configuration.

07 — Data Processing & ML Pipeline

From raw radar returns to lung volume

The radar sees a scene full of reflections: chest, bed surface, floor, equipment. The pipeline's job is to strip everything away except the displacement of the chest wall, then convert that displacement signal into clinically meaningful spirometry values.

01
Range FFT & Gating
A Fast Fourier Transform on the beat frequency separates every object in the scene by distance. A range gate selects only the highest-moving peak (the patient's torso at 0.5–1.0 m) and zeros all other reflections, including the bed frame and floor.
signal
02
Phase Unwrapping
During a forced exhale, the chest can collapse faster than one quarter-wavelength per chirp (0.975 mm), causing the phase to "wrap" around 2π and produce a jagged, meaningless signal. Itoh's algorithm adds or subtracts multiples of 2π to maintain a continuous, smooth displacement curve through the explosive exhalation onset.
signal
03
Advanced Filtering
Bandpass filtering at 0.1–3 Hz removes electronic noise and slow postural drift. An adaptive notch filter then identifies the 1–2 Hz ballistocardiogram (the heartbeat's mechanical "thump" on the chest surface) and subtracts it, preventing the cardiac pulse from artificially inflating the FVC volume estimate.
filter
04
Feature Extraction
The displacement waveform is decomposed into clinical features: area under the curve (AUC) maps to FVC; the slope of the first 200 ms corresponds to Peak Expiratory Flow (PEF); and the rib-cage to abdominal contribution ratio provides a positional correction input for the inference model.
features
05
XGBoost Inference
A gradient-boosted tree model takes radar features alongside patient anthropometrics (age, sex, height, weight) and a supine positional correction factor. A 5 cm chest displacement in a tall young adult represents a very different lung volume than the same displacement in an elderly patient; the model accounts for this.
ML
06
ATS/ERS Quality Scoring
Every maneuver is scored against 2019 ATS/ERS guidelines. The plateau criterion checks whether displacement stopped changing for at least 1 second, confirming complete exhalation. The Vmax–Vmin velocity delta validates obstruction grade before a result is accepted and displayed.
QC

The velocity-differential diagnostic insight

A high Vmax–Vmin delta (the gap between peak chest-wall speed at exhalation onset and the deceleration plateau) correlates with healthy, unobstructed airways and high FEV1. A compressed delta is the radar fingerprint of obstructive disease (COPD, asthma). Measuring the differential rather than absolute displacement makes results less sensitive to inter-session positioning variance, improving repeatability without requiring the patient to maintain an identical position across visits.

08 — Clinical Application

Staging lung disease and triaging patients

The system is designed for longitudinal monitoring: tracking the trend of a patient's lung health over weeks or months. A single reading may carry a margin of error; a consistent downward trend is a powerful and actionable clinical signal. Results are mapped to the GOLD classification, the international standard for COPD staging, based on FEV1 as a percentage of the expected value for a patient's age, height, and sex.

📈

Automated acute exacerbation alert

If a patient's FEV1 drops between GOLD stages within a 48-hour window, the system triggers an automated alert without waiting for a scheduled clinic appointment. In home or ICU settings, this can be the difference between a medication adjustment and an emergency admission.

🔁

Paradoxical breathing detection

The radar observes the entire torso simultaneously. When the rib cage and abdomen move out of phase (thoraco-abdominal paradox), it signals respiratory muscle fatigue and impending failure. This early warning appears before FEV1 reaches a critical threshold and before any conventional contact sensor would register a problem.

👴

Geriatric passive monitoring

Elderly bedridden patients are monitored without effort or travel. The system flags a 15% FEV1 decline (the standard clinical significance threshold) and notifies the supervising physician, enabling a pharmacological adjustment such as increasing bronchodilator dosage before the patient deteriorates enough to require hospitalisation.

👶

Pediatric tracking without co-operation

Children are tracked by deviation from their individual Best Personal Displacement baseline. No mouthpiece, no forced maneuver instruction needed. A child can lie still and watch a screen while the radar passively captures lung motion, opening early-stage asthma and cystic fibrosis monitoring to the youngest age groups.

Strategic positioning

The established spirometry companies have no incentive to build a product that eliminates their consumable revenue stream. By focusing on contactless measurement, the device avoids direct competition entirely. As automotive radar demand continues to drive down the unit cost of the TI IWR6843 chip, the bill of materials for the sensing box will fall year on year, making it progressively more accessible to primary health centres and home-care providers across India and South-East Asia.

11 — Future Scope

What comes after the prototype

The current system establishes the core sensing and processing pipeline for supine patients. The roadmap extends it toward anatomical precision, continuous passive monitoring, and full integration with hospital digital infrastructure.

📡

Dual-radar two-compartment model

Separate thoracic and abdominal units for independent rib-cage and diaphragm tracking. Enables detection of thoraco-abdominal paradox that a single sensor may miss in borderline cases.

near-term hardware
🌙

Passive continuous monitoring

Low-power firmware mode tracks tidal volume trends and respiratory rate variability while the patient sleeps. 77 GHz phase sensitivity enables non-invasive OSA detection (chest effort present, airflow absent).

Separate models for pediatrics (high respiratory rate, compliant rib cage), geriatrics (senile emphysema), and disease-specific chest geometries such as COPD barrel chest and ILD shallow cycling patterns.

Predictive exacerbation AI

CNN/RNN deep learning on months of continuous breathing data. Goal: 48-hour advance prediction of COPD exacerbations and asthma attacks before symptom onset, enabling preventative medication adjustment.

long-term AI
🔗

Towards a multi-parameter bedside monitor

Modern medical equipment increasingly consolidates multiple diagnostic functions into a single platform. A ventilator tracks pressure and flow; a patient monitor reads ECG, SpO2, and temperature simultaneously. This device should evolve in the same direction. The same 77 GHz radar that measures chest wall displacement can simultaneously extract heart rate variability (from ballistocardiogram signals already present in the pipeline), respiratory rate, and coarse body movement for fall detection. With a secondary physiological sensor layer (e.g. a low-cost SpO2 module and skin temperature patch), the device becomes a non-contact vital-signs hub in addition to a PFT system. The long-term vision is a single ceiling-mounted unit that replaces multiple bedside instruments in ICU and home-care settings, reducing equipment footprint, cabling, and the cognitive burden on nursing staff.

References & how they underpin this work

This project is not based on a single prior work. It is a deliberate synthesis of independent research threads, each contributing a specific layer of the design. The table below maps each source to the part of our system it directly informs.

  1. Droitcour AD et al. Range correlation in single-chip Doppler radars for non-contact cardiopulmonary monitoring. IEEE Trans Microwave Theory Tech. 2004;52(3):838–848. Foundational proof that phase-shift radar can resolve sub-millimetre cardiopulmonary motion at a distance. Provides the theoretical basis for our entire sensing approach and the x(t) = λφ(t)/4π displacement equation.
  2. Lv H et al. FMCW-radar-based respiratory monitoring and lung function estimation. IEEE Access. 2020;8:105272–105283. Directly validates that FMCW radar (the modulation type used by the TI IWR6843) can estimate lung function parameters from chest wall displacement. Informs our choice of FMCW over CW radar and our Range FFT gating pipeline design.
  3. Wang G et al. Simultaneous heart rate and respiration rate monitoring using FMCW radar. IEEE Sensors J. 2020;20(10):5698–5707. Demonstrates that heartbeat and breathing signals can be separated from the same radar return. Directly informs our adaptive notch filter design in pipeline Step 03, which subtracts the ballistocardiogram from the FVC displacement trace.
  4. Massagram W et al. Tidal volume estimation via Doppler radar. IEEE EMBC. 2008:4735–4738. Shows that radar displacement AUC correlates with tidal volume. This is the evidence base for our displacement AUC → FVC mapping in the feature extraction stage (Step 04).
  5. Konno K & Mead J. Separate volume changes of rib cage and abdomen during breathing. J Appl Physiol. 1967;22(3):407–422. The classic two-compartment model of breathing. Establishes that total lung volume can be decomposed into independent rib-cage and abdominal contributions, which is the physiological rationale for our RC/AB ratio feature and our future dual-radar design.
  6. Graham BL et al. Standardization of spirometry 2019 update. Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2019;200(8):e70–e88. The current international standard for spirometry acceptability and repeatability criteria. Our ATS/ERS quality scoring module (pipeline Step 06) is directly built against this document: the plateau criterion, the Vmax–Vmin delta, and the maneuver acceptance thresholds all derive from these guidelines.
  7. Vogelmeier CF et al. Global strategy for COPD – GOLD 2017. Eur Respir J. 2017;49(3):1700214. Defines the GOLD staging thresholds (GOLD 1–4) used by our clinical triage engine. The exacerbation alert logic and the 30% / 50% / 80% FEV1 cutoffs in our system are taken directly from this document.
  8. Quanjer PH et al. Multi-ethnic reference values for spirometry: GLI-2012. Eur Respir J. 2012;40(6):1324–1343. Provides the multi-ethnic anthropometric reference equations used to compute FEV1 % predicted from height, age, sex, and ethnicity. This is what the XGBoost model uses as its ground-truth target during training, and what allows the system to account for the Indian patient population specifically.
  9. ISO 26782:2009. Spirometers intended for measurement of time forced expired volumes – performance requirements. Sets the accuracy and repeatability tolerances any spirometer must meet to be clinically valid. Our quality scoring module is designed to produce results that fall within ISO 26782 limits, establishing a clear certification pathway for the device.
  10. IndiaMART, RespBuy, Biomed Suppliers. Spirometer and consumable pricing data, India market. Accessed June 2026. Source for all device and per-unit consumable prices used in the cost-of-ownership comparison (Section 09).
