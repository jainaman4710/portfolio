Exploratory Analysis

Key findings from the data

Eight significant observations surfaced during EDA, directly shaping the design of both revenue solutions.

01

Weekly demand valleys

Below-average bookings in Week 3 of May and Weeks 1 & 4 of June — likely seasonal weather patterns rather than pricing or operational causes.

02

Cancellations mirror booking trends

Cancellations track the overall booking curve closely with no anomalous spikes, suggesting systematic rather than event-driven churn across the portfolio.

03

Mumbai leads, Delhi lags

Mumbai holds the highest booking volume and the most hotels. Delhi is at the opposite end. No city shows disproportionate cancellation surges relative to its booking volume.

04

60% refund — the core leakage signal

All cancelled bookings refund exactly 60% uniformly — a static policy regardless of timing. Revenue ratio is binary: 1.0 for checkouts/no-shows, 0.4 for cancellations.

05

Ratings missing for ~40% of stays

Approximately 60% of completed stays received a rating. Cancelled and no-show bookings produce zero ratings — no imputation needed for the primary analysis.

06

Same-day bookings dominate

Days-booked-in-advance is heavily right-skewed: mode = 0. Most bookings cluster in the final 7 days — a critical input for the lead-time variable in dynamic pricing.

07

Three natural performance tiers

Scatter of average occupancy vs. mean rating reveals three clusters: 4 underperformers (<50% occ, <2.5★), 9 mid-tier, and 12 top-tier (>60% occ, >4.0★).

08

Revenue leakage is policy-driven, not platform-driven

All platforms, cities, and properties show the exact same 0.4/1.0 revenue ratio split. The leakage is entirely a function of the cancellation policy — not the booking channel.

Revenue Solutions

Two levers, one framework

Two independent but complementary interventions designed, back-tested, and quantified — plus a portfolio management strategy based on the performance tiers.

The existing policy returns 60% on every cancellation regardless of timing — leaving revenue on the table for last-minute cancellations while offering little flexibility incentive to advance planners. Three replacement policies were evaluated using estimated cancellation lead times.

Why Policy 2?

Policy 1 generates the highest raw revenue (+9.24%) by retaining 90% on last-minute cancellations — but it also scores the lowest on customer satisfaction (4.72/10), meaning aggressive revenue extraction comes at a real cost to guest relationships. Policy 3 improves slightly on satisfaction (4.94/10) but surrenders revenue (+7.43%). Policy 2 is the strategic sweet spot: it earns slightly less than Policy 1 in raw terms (+7.65%) but scores the highest on satisfaction (5.21/10) — the only policy where both metrics move in a favorable direction together. The graduated structure also naturally aligns with booking behavior, rewarding advance planners with 80% refunds while deterring last-minute cancellations through 80% revenue retention.

A tri-variable dynamic pricing model adjusts the base room rate in real time based on three demand signals derived directly from the booking data.

Variable 1 — Occupancy Rate Deviation

Price adjusts based on how far current occupancy for a property-room-date combination deviates from its historical average. High occupancy triggers price increases to capture willingness-to-pay; low occupancy triggers discounts to stimulate fill.

deviation = (current_occ − avg_occ) / avg_occ

Variable 2 — Day Type (Weekend/Weekday)

Weekends attract a natural demand premium. The model captures this explicitly as a multiplier rather than letting it stay implicit in the occupancy average, ensuring weekday discounts also run independently to maintain occupancy momentum on slower days.

day_mult = 1.10 (weekend) | 0.95 (weekday)

Variable 3 — Booking Lead Time

Lead time is a powerful proxy for demand urgency and booking certainty. The logic is behavioral: a guest booking today for tonight is price-inelastic — they need the room and have few alternatives. A guest booking 3 weeks out has time to compare and is sensitive to price. The model exploits this asymmetry in three bands:

Lead TimeFactorRationale
0–3 days× 1.15Scarcity premium — high urgency, low alternatives
4–14 days× 1.00Neutral — standard booking window, base rate applies
15+ days× 0.90Early-bird incentive — reduces uncertainty, locks in revenue
final_price = base × (1 + occ_factor) × day_mult × lead_factor
