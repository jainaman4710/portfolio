01 · Introduction

Boredom: An Unpleasant Necessity

Boredom is the unpleasant feeling during idle time when cognitive resources are under-utilised due to a lack of stimulation. It creates a restless urge for something more.

Why scrolling, not sketching? Because the mind seeks the path of least resistance. Passive consumption demands little. True creation asks for effort: for us to actively shape the quiet. A harder, yet richer, path to take.

Why Now?

The consequences (mental fatigue, loss of focus, stifled creativity) are no longer niche problems; they define the modern experience. If we don't consciously design ways to bring back restorative pauses now, the trend towards hyper-stimulation risks fundamentally altering our capacity for deep thought and genuine innovation.

02 · The Concept

The Creation of Mirage

Remember those moments when you're travelling? Instead of looking at your phone, you find yourself just staring out the window at the passing scenery, letting your mind drift.

That specific "zone-out" feeling: that effortless pause where your mind isn't actively working, just quietly observing. This was our core inspiration. We designed Mirage to recreate that moment of calm, quiet reflection and make it part of your regular life.

Detects crowd presence and adjusts visual intensity dynamically based on occupancy.

Characteristics of the Display

Clouds drift, sunlight dapples, and water ripples evolve over minutes to keep visuals low-demand.

Soft washes of complementary hues blend extremely gradually, avoiding sharp edges or rapid transitions.

Simple lines and shapes rotate, expand, or contract smoothly to maintain a meditative flow.

Soft caustics, gentle lens flares, and volumetric light beams shift imperceptibly without overstimulation.

Organic forms, branching structures, cellular motifs, and topographic lines slowly morph, leveraging nature's calming geometry.

Self-similar patterns inspired by leaf veins and coastlines provide visual complexity without cognitive overload.

Greens, blues, and earth tones mimic outdoor environments to reinforce biophilic benefits.

All movements follow long, non-repetitive cycles to prevent habituation and sustain quiet interest.

Visuals avoid recognisable objects or storytelling elements. Attention remains at ease, never drawn into active engagement.

Unintended Consequences

Desensitisation Effect

Repeated exposure could normalise abstract visuals, reducing the zoning-out impact over time.

Accessibility Gaps

Visually impaired or colour-blind individuals may not perceive the intended calming effect.

Space Behaviour Alteration

People might unconsciously linger longer in certain areas due to the nature of the visuals.
