01 / Problem Statement

Railway safety demands better than manual inspection

Train derailments, often caused by undetected track cracks, endanger passengers, workers, and nearby communities. The existing maintenance model relies on human walkthroughs that are shift-bound, error-prone, and unable to detect microfractures.

⚠

The core risk

Cracks in railway tracks develop from mechanical stress, thermal expansion, material fatigue, and corrosion. Small and subsurface fractures go undetected by visual inspection, eventually compromising structural integrity and risking derailment.

📡

Our approach

Infrared thermal imaging sensors scan tracks continuously, generating real-time thermograms. Since cracks alter thermal conductivity, they produce detectable temperature variations, even for microfractures invisible to the naked eye.

Figure : Progression from mechanical stress to derailment risk in railway tracks

Current manual method : the Keyman system

A Keyman patrols a designated section of track, visually inspecting for anomalies. Work is done in two shifts (6–11 am and 2–5 pm), covering approximately 4 km of track. Despite the use of tools, this system is shift-constrained and unable to detect subsurface defects.

02 / Crack Types & Detection

Four key defect types, all thermographically detectable

Through simulation of common rail defects, thermal imaging was validated as capable of detecting structural anomalies that visual inspection misses entirely.

01

Transverse Cracks

Form perpendicular to the rail's length from internal defects, thermal stress, or excessive wheel loads. Particularly dangerous as they propagate rapidly, risking sudden rail fractures. Simulated for thermal stress-induced failure and crack propagation patterns.

02

Head Checks

Small surface cracks at the rail head caused by repeated wheel-rail contact stress. Common on curved tracks and high-braking sections. Simulated to analyse localised overheating at rail-head contact points, revealing thermal hotspots.

03

Squats

Dark depressions formed from repetitive impact loading and plastic deformation. Begin as surface defects, expanding into deeper cracks over time. Modelled to study heat dissipation changes from plastic deformation and impact-related data.

04

Surface Spalling

Small surface fragments chip away due to material fatigue, corrosion, or stress concentration. Examined through heat loss characteristics in worn rail sections, showing distinct thermal signatures detectable by IR.

Simulation findings

Thermal imaging highlighted anomalies that were difficult to detect through visual inspection, including internal subsurface cracks with distinct heat signatures.

Temperature distribution analysis provided early warnings for potential failures before any visible damage occurred, enabling proactive maintenance.

The method proved effective in low-light and challenging environmental conditions, where traditional optical inspections are unreliable or impossible.

Internal cracks showed distinct heat signatures, confirming that thermal sensors can detect subsurface defects beyond the capability of surface-level techniques.

03 / Product

RailSafe Thermal Inspector : RSIT

An advanced non-destructive testing (NDT) system that uses infrared thermography to detect surface and subsurface defects by analysing thermal radiation emitted from railway tracks. Contactless, high-speed, and suitable for large rail networks.

Figure : End-to-end RSIT system architecture: from IR sensor capture to maintenance action

Working principle

The system captures thermal radiation naturally emitted by tracks. Structural defects, cracks, fatigue spots, delaminations, alter thermal conductivity and emissivity, creating detectable temperature variations. A high-sensitivity IR detector generates thermograms (heat maps), which are analysed to detect and classify defects early.

Cooling mechanism

Stable operation is ensured through a three-layer thermal management system:

Active airflowMini DC fan · 12V · 40–60mm
Passive dissipationAluminium / copper heatsink
Active cooling (extreme)TEC1-12706 Peltier module

04 / Implementation & Future Expansion

A phased rollout from pilot to national scale

Deployment begins with high-risk testing zones before scaling to full rail network coverage, with AI-driven analysis improving continuously through real-world data.

Figure : Three-phase deployment roadmap from prototype to full-scale national deployment
Phase 1
Prototype & Pilot
0–6 months · 10–50 units/mo
Small-batch production for pilot testing on high-risk zones: high-speed corridors, extreme temperature regions, heavy freight routes, and historically accident-prone sections. Focus on design optimisation and sensor calibration.
Phase 2
Initial Mass Production
6–12 months · 200–500 units/mo
Ramp-up after successful testing. Deployment on railway maintenance vehicles and limited train routes. Supply chain partnerships established for consistent availability of IR sensors and electronic components.
Phase 3
Full-Scale Deployment
Beyond 1 year · 1,000+ units/mo
Sensors installed on operational passenger and freight trains for continuous real-time monitoring. Automated assembly lines improve production efficiency. Potential expansion to international railway networks.
1.3

AI-based data processing

Machine learning algorithms analyse continuous thermograms to differentiate actual cracks from surface imperfections, minimise false positives, and categorise severity as minor, moderate, or severe for timely intervention.

1.4

Control centre integration

Cloud-based connection to centralised railway control centres enables automated alerts to maintenance teams, predictive maintenance scheduling, and long-term historical wear pattern analysis.

1.5

Calibration & environmental adaptation

Sensors calibrated for dust, humidity, and surface emissivity variations. Algorithms refined to distinguish real cracks from misleading thermal variations. Material-specific calibration for different track compositions.

2.0

Automated maintenance scheduling

Integration with railway maintenance planning tools enables automatic repair scheduling. Government and private sector collaboration ensures smooth adoption, funding, and regulatory approvals for nationwide rollout.

06 / Conclusion

Redefining railway maintenance for the future

RSIT represents a shift from reactive maintenance to proactive, data-driven track monitoring, one that scales across India's vast rail network and sets a model for global railway safety infrastructure.

🔍

Early & accurate detection

Identifies microfractures and subsurface defects invisible to manual inspection, enabling intervention before failures escalate to derailments.

🤖

Reduced human dependency

Eliminates reliance on individual expertise and shift-based patrol, replacing it with continuous automated monitoring free from human error.

📊

Predictive capability

By analysing thermograms over time, the system can identify areas at risk of future structural failure, extending beyond detection to prediction.

⚡

Non-intrusive monitoring

Contactless thermal scanning continues without disrupting train operations. No additional inspection runs or service interruptions required.

🌐

Scalable deployment

From 10 pilot units to 1,000+ per month, the modular system scales with demand and supports nationwide and international rollout.

🧠

AI-driven evolution

Machine learning models improve continuously from real-world data, making the system more accurate over time and adaptable to new environments.
